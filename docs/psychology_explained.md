# Ad Psychology Explained

## Pattern Interrupt
Break expectations to grab attention in the first 0.3–1.5 seconds.
The brain registers anything unexpected and pauses to process it.
Use: Unexpected visuals, contradictory statements, mid-sentence openings.

## Curiosity Gap
Create an unanswered question to increase retention.
Based on the Zeigarnik Effect — the brain compulsively wants to close open loops.
Use: "The #1 reason [x] doesn't work is NOT what you think..."

## Pain Amplification
Deepen the emotional cost of the problem before offering the solution.
People feel losses 2x more than equivalent gains (Kahneman & Tversky).
Use: Quantify the cost — time wasted, money spent, confidence lost.

## Loss Aversion
Highlight what users lose by not acting, rather than what they gain by acting.
"Stop losing $200/month" outperforms "Save $200/month."
Use: Frame inaction as the real cost.

## Identity Alignment
Align the product with who they want to become, not just what they want to do.
People buy products that reflect the identity they aspire to.
Use: "This is for people who refuse to settle for average."

## Contrast Principle
The brain judges things relative to what came before.
Show the before to make the after feel dramatic.
Use: Side-by-side comparisons, before/after reveals, old vs. new solutions.

## Social Proof
When uncertain, humans look to others for guidance.
Specific numbers outperform vague claims ("10,000 customers" > "many customers").
Use: Reviews, user counts, expert endorsements, testimonial quotes.

## Cognitive Ease
Simple language, short sentences, and familiar patterns reduce mental effort.
Low-friction content is processed faster and trusted more.
Use: Write like you talk. Max 10 words per sentence in the script.
